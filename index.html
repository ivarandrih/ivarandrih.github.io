San Francisco Crime Data Analysis, 2003 - 2025

Where Crime Concentrates: A Look at Drug Offenses in San Francisco

Crime in San Francisco is not evenly distributed. This short data story explores how crime profiles differ across police districts, why drug offenses stand out geographically especially in the tenderloin district, and how those patterns persist over time.

Introduction

San Francisco is often discussed as if crime were a single city-wide pattern, but the data shows something more uneven. Different police districts have very different crime profiles, meaning that some crime types occur much more frequently in certain places than would be expected from the city average.

This analysis focuses on drug offenses. It begins by comparing how different types of crime are over- or under-represented across districts. It then takes a closer look at the geographic distribution of drug offenses, before examining how these district-level patterns evolve over time.

1. Crime profiles differ across districts

The figure below compares crime types across San Francisco police districts using the ratio P(crime | district) / P(crime). This measure allows us to understand how common a given crime type is within a district relative to its overall frequency across the city. A value of 1 indicates that a crime occurs at the city-wide average, values above 1 indicate that the crime is over-represented in that district, and values below 1 indicate under-representation.

This normalization is important because districts differ greatly in their total number of reported incidents. By adjusting for overall crime frequency, we can make meaningful comparisons between districts of very different sizes and activity levels. Rather than simply showing where more crime happens, this approach highlights where specific types of crime are unusually common or rare.

The chart reveals that crime profiles are far from uniform across the city. Instead, distinct patterns emerge: certain crime types cluster strongly within particular districts, while others are relatively absent. This suggests that crime in San Francisco is shaped by local geographic, social, and economic factors rather than being evenly distributed across neighborhoods.

Figure 1. Crime type over- and under-representation by police district. Values above 1 indicate that a crime occurs more frequently in that district than in the city overall.

2. Drug offenses stand out geographically

Among the crime types shown in the district profile chart, drug offenses stand out as particularly uneven across San Francisco. To highlight this pattern more clearly, the next map focuses specifically on the district-level ratio P(drug offense | district) / P(drug offense), allowing us to isolate where drug-related incidents are unusually common relative to the city-wide average.

A strong spatial pattern immediately emerges. The Tenderloin stands out sharply, with drug offenses occurring at rates far above the city average. In contrast, most other districts show values at or below 1, indicating that drug-related incidents are less common relative to the overall distribution of crime in those areas.

Rather than being evenly distributed across the city, drug offenses appear highly concentrated in a small number of locations. This suggests that local factors — such as population density, housing conditions, policing practices, or the presence of open-air drug markets — may play a significant role in shaping where these incidents are recorded.

The geographic concentration observed here aligns with widely reported patterns in San Francisco, where the Tenderloin has long been associated with visible street-level drug activity. At the same time, it is important to note that police data reflects enforcement and reporting practices as much as underlying behavior, meaning these patterns should be interpreted with caution.

Figure 2. Geographic distribution of drug offense over- and under-representation across San Francisco police districts. Warmer colors indicate districts where drug offenses occur more frequently than the city-wide average.

The map turns the abstract ratio into something easier to read spatially: some districts are simply much more associated with drug offense reports than others.

3. The concentration persists over time

Geographic concentration alone does not tell us whether the pattern is stable. The interactive chart below adds time, showing how district-level drug offense over-representation changes across years.

By pressing play or moving the slider, the reader can see whether the same districts remain above average or whether the pattern shifts over time. This adds an exploratory layer to the story while still staying focused on the same central question.

Figure 3. Drug offense over- and under-representation by district over time. The dashed line at 1 marks the city-wide average.

Conclusion

Taken together, these three figures show that crime in San Francisco is not just uneven in volume, but uneven in type. Districts differ in what crimes are relatively common there, and drug offenses in particular appear concentrated in specific parts of the city.

The Tenderloin stands out both geographically and over time, suggesting that this is not a one-year anomaly but a persistent pattern. At the same time, these figures should be interpreted carefully: reported crime reflects not only underlying behavior, but also policing practices, reporting, and broader social conditions.
