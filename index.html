San Francisco Crime Data Analysis, 2003 - 2025

Where Crime Concentrates: A Look at Drug Offenses in San Francisco

Crime in San Francisco is not evenly distributed. This short data story explores how crime profiles differ across police districts, why drug offenses stand out geographically, and how those patterns persist over time.

Introduction

San Francisco is often discussed as if crime were a single city-wide pattern, but the data shows something more uneven. Different police districts have very different crime profiles, meaning that some crime types occur much more frequently in certain places than would be expected from the city average.

In this analysis, I focus on drug offenses. First, I compare how crime types are over- or under-represented across districts. Then I zoom in on drug offenses geographically, and finally I show how that district pattern changes over time.

1. Crime profiles differ across districts

The figure below compares crime types across San Francisco police districts using the ratio P(crime | district) / P(crime). A value of 1 means the crime occurs at the city-wide average. Values above 1 indicate over-representation, while values below 1 indicate under-representation.

This makes it possible to compare districts fairly, even when they have different total numbers of reported incidents. The chart shows that district crime profiles are not uniform: some offenses cluster strongly in particular districts.

Figure 1. Crime type over- and under-representation by police district. Values above 1 indicate that a crime occurs more frequently in that district than in the city overall.

2. Drug offenses stand out geographically

Among the crime types in the district profile chart, drug offenses are especially uneven. To make that clearer, the next map focuses only on the district-level ratio for drug offenses.

A clear spatial concentration appears: the Tenderloin stands out sharply compared with most other districts. Rather than being spread evenly across San Francisco, drug offenses appear concentrated in a few areas.

Figure 2. Geographic distribution of drug offense over- and under-representation across San Francisco police districts. Warmer colors indicate districts where drug offenses occur more frequently than the city-wide average.

The map turns the abstract ratio into something easier to read spatially: some districts are simply much more associated with drug offense reports than others.

3. The concentration persists over time

Geographic concentration alone does not tell us whether the pattern is stable. The interactive chart below adds time, showing how district-level drug offense over-representation changes across years.

By pressing play or moving the slider, the reader can see whether the same districts remain above average or whether the pattern shifts over time. This adds an exploratory layer to the story while still staying focused on the same central question.

Figure 3. Drug offense over- and under-representation by district over time. The dashed line at 1 marks the city-wide average.

Conclusion

Taken together, these three figures show that crime in San Francisco is not just uneven in volume, but uneven in type. Districts differ in what crimes are relatively common there, and drug offenses in particular appear concentrated in specific parts of the city.

The Tenderloin stands out both geographically and over time, suggesting that this is not a one-year anomaly but a persistent pattern. At the same time, these figures should be interpreted carefully: reported crime reflects not only underlying behavior, but also policing practices, reporting, and broader social conditions.
